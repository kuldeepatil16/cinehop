# Spanish cinema-goers are frustrated, and no one is solving it

**Spain's major cinema chains deliver some of the worst digital experiences in European entertainment.** Cinesa and Yelmo — the country's two largest chains — hold Trustpilot ratings of **1.4/5 and 1.3/5** respectively, with over 80% one-star reviews. The complaints are not edge cases: they reveal systematic failures in booking technology, customer service, VOSE discoverability, and pricing transparency. Most critically for CineHop's thesis, **no cross-chain aggregator or comparison platform exists in Spain** — there is no Fandango equivalent. Users must manually check multiple apps and websites to compare showtimes, formats, prices, and VOSE availability, each requiring separate account creation. The evidence strongly validates a real, unmet need.

---

## The digital booking experience is fundamentally broken

Both Cinesa and Yelmo apps suffer from the same catastrophic failure pattern: **they charge users' cards but fail to deliver tickets.** This is the single most repeated complaint across Trustpilot, OCU (Spain's consumer organization), and TripAdvisor.

A Cinesa user (September 2025) described the cycle: *"Te hacen el cargo en la tarjeta y te dicen que la compra no se ha producido por problemas en la web. Como no recibes las entradas en tu mail, vuelves a realizar la compra... Resultado: VARIAS COMPRAS, NINGUNA ENTRADA Y LOS CARGOS EN LA TARJETA."* ("They charge your card and tell you the purchase didn't go through. Since you don't receive tickets by email, you try again... Result: MULTIPLE PURCHASES, NO TICKETS, AND ALL THE CHARGES ON YOUR CARD.")

A Yelmo user reported the same: *"Utilicé la aplicación de Yelmo cines para comprar unas entradas y falló dos veces pero al final cobró las entradas y no recibí el correo de confirmación. Llevo infinidad de correos con la oficina de atención al cliente y me están dando largas."* ("I used the Yelmo app to buy tickets and it failed twice but charged me anyway with no confirmation email. I've sent endless emails to customer service and they keep stalling.")

Beyond phantom charges, both apps exhibit a bizarre pattern of **auto-selecting the wrong cinema or wrong date.** Cinesa's app assigned a user in Asturias to a Madrid cinema after a forced update. Yelmo's app repeatedly defaults to today's date even when users explicitly select a future date — confirmed by a formal OCU complaint: *"La aplicación móvil de Yelmo no está bien desarrollada, y cuando compras una entrada a una fecha elegida que no sea la de hoy, automáticamente al entrar en la cartelera te elige la de hoy."* Yelmo's website also randomly switches the cinema filter mid-purchase, sending one user's tickets to a cinema 45 minutes away.

Cinesa's web platform drew this summary from one user: *"La peor experiencia digital que jamás haya tenido. La web no funciona. La App tampoco. Da igual el terminal que uses."* ("The worst digital experience I've ever had. The website doesn't work. The app doesn't work either. Doesn't matter what device you use.") After a migration to MyCinesa, the platform broke further — another user reported: *"Desde la aplicación móvil se puede hacer más bien poco porque te lleva directamente a la web, que funciona mal en todos los sentidos, la experiencia de usuario es nefasta y uno ya no se atreve a comprar nada a través de ella."*

When things go wrong, **customer service is effectively nonexistent.** Neither chain offers functioning phone support. Cinesa's virtual assistant doesn't activate. Yelmo's 902 premium-rate number has charged users up to €75 in phone bills without resolving issues. Email responses take days to weeks. One Yelmo user described a Kafkaesque refund process: *"Solo ponen pegas... ahora me han pedido que firme un documento con hora de la compra, día, fecha del cine, nombre del cine, DNI, nombre completo, IBAN, teléfono, correo... está claro que está hecho para que la gente se canse de esperar y Yelmo se quede con ese dinero."* Both chains enforce rigid **no-refund, no-exchange policies** even when their own technology causes the error.

## VOSE is a discovery nightmare with no unified solution

Spain's dubbing tradition — rooted in Franco's 1941 Language Defence Law — means **only ~5.7% of the country's ~3,500 screens show films in original version.** Of 94 million annual cinema-goers, barely 4 million choose VOSE. But demand is growing rapidly, driven by streaming habits and Spain's **6.9 million foreign residents** (14.1% of the population as of 2025).

The core problem is **discoverability and scarcity.** An American expat blog captured it precisely: *"Depending on where you are in Spain, the film you want to see may only be available once throughout the week in the VOSE version."* On the Costa Brava, VOSE screenings happen "on the second and fourth Thursdays of the month." In Gandia (Valencia), an expat posted: *"My local cinema only shows films in Spanish. I've tried googling for local cinemas that do original language screenings, but so far no luck."* Recommendations included traveling to Jávea — an hour away — just to watch a movie in English.

Even in Madrid and Barcelona, VOSE is treated as niche. One expat noted with amusement: *"It was so funny to think that seeing the movie in English was the 'indie'/'hipster'/'cultural' way of doing it in Spain."* VOSE screenings are often relegated to small, poorly maintained arthouse cinemas. A review of Cines Verdi Madrid (a dedicated VOSE cinema) was titled "LOS PEORES CINES VOSE DE MADRID" and described: *"Con la sala llena, la ventilación es horrorosa, además de hacer un calor de espanto... las butacas están viejas y obsoletas."*

**VOSE labeling itself is unreliable.** In February 2026, a Yelmo customer discovered only upon arrival that their session was VOSE — it wasn't marked at purchase, wasn't on the ticket, and Yelmo refused a refund: *"En el momento de la compra en ningún lado aparecía que esa sesión era VOSE. En las entradas que tengo tampoco aparece por ningún lado."* The reverse also occurs — users expecting VOSE arrive to find a dubbed screening.

The current discovery landscape is fragmented across disconnected tools:

- **Mabuse.es** lists VOSE showtimes but covers Madrid only
- **SensaCine and eCartelera** show cross-chain showtimes but cannot sell tickets — they redirect to individual chain websites
- **Each chain has its own VOSE page** (Cinesa, Yelmo, Kinépolis) requiring separate checking
- **Guía del Ocio** offers a "VO" filter but is a general entertainment site, not cinema-focused
- **Expat blogs** (Sincerely Spain, Valencia Life, ShMadrid) serve as informal VOSE directories but quickly become outdated

No single tool provides **cross-chain, cross-city, real-time VOSE discovery with booking capability.** No tool sends alerts when a specific film becomes available in VOSE. And many cinema websites don't even offer English-language interfaces — ironic given VOSE's core audience includes non-Spanish speakers. NjOY Costa Brava noted: *"On some websites, an issue appears to occur when purchasing tickets using the English language version."*

## Pricing opacity and hidden fees fuel deep resentment

Ticket prices vary by up to **148% across Spain** (FACUA study), from €4.50 in Soria to **€10.90** at Yelmo Plenilunio and Cinesa Manoteras in Madrid. There is no easy way for consumers to compare prices across chains — another gap an aggregator could fill.

"Gastos de gestión" (service fees) are a particular sore point. An OCU study found **92% of entertainment companies charge them, adding an average 11% surcharge.** OCU called them *"clearly unjustified"* — *"si es el mismo usuario quien selecciona la fecha, el asiento, introduce el método de pago... ¿qué gestión hace el vendedor?"* ("If the user selects the date, seat, enters payment... what management does the seller do?") The Spanish Ministry of Consumer Affairs issued formal guidance in July 2024 requiring fees to be included in displayed prices, and an investigation into a major ticketing company for *"presuntas irregularidades"* was underway as of May 2025.

Cinesa's pricing drew particular ire for layered surcharges on top of subscriptions. One pass holder: *"Tengo un bono de lunes a jueves para cinco entradas y si la sala es grande te cobran 1,5 euros aparte."* Cinesa Unlimited's price rose from €15.90 to **€17.90/month** in October 2025 — and users discovered that during Black Friday 2025, Cinesa raised the annual price from €149 to €179 and then offered a "€30 discount" back to the original price, which users called *"deshonesto."*

Concession prices generate visceral reactions. One Cinesa user in Santiago de Compostela detailed the gap: *"En Cines Compostela, un menú grande cuesta 5€, mientras que en Cinesa un menú algo mayor sube a 9,20–9,90€, prácticamente el doble."* Another compared it to dining: *"En el bar de al lado de mi tienda, primer plato, segundo plato, postre, bebida y café: 9€. En estos cines, palomitas pequeñas y bebida pequeña..."* The resentment has spawned memes — *"Cuando sea millonario me compraré una entrada para el cine, palomitas y un refresco grande"* ("When I'm a millionaire I'll buy myself a cinema ticket, popcorn, and a large drink") is a recurring joke on Spanish social media.

## Expats face compounded frustrations beyond dubbing

For Spain's nearly 9 million foreign-born residents, cinema problems stack up. The dubbing shock comes first — as ShMadrid described: *"All of the sudden you are listening to Clint Eastwood speaking in fluent Spanish with a completely different, odd speaking voice you have never heard before."* Valencia Life's expat writer was blunter: *"My family and I found it damn near impossible to find undubbed movies. Being a family of cinephiles, this dubbing brought untold horrors into our inner circle."*

But the problems extend beyond dubbing. Kiosk-only ticketing at some Yelmo locations offers **no language option beyond Spanish**, leaving non-speakers stranded. A foreign tourist in Seville described the experience: *"They had a touch pad ticketing with nobody manning the desk. It was only in Spanish... I said I speak French, English, Portuguese, Dutch but habla poco español. She shrugged her shoulder and went back to twirling her hair. So now instead of watching a movie I'm writing this review. My son is bored."*

Seat assignment catches newcomers off-guard: *"Little did we realize, here in Spain tickets are sold with a seat number and when our friends arrived just 10 minutes later it was impossible for them to get seats near us!"* The typical expat workaround involves consulting blog posts, checking multiple cinema websites, and sometimes traveling long distances — as one forum user recommended commuting from Gandia to Jávea (40+ minutes) for English-language screenings.

## Recurring patterns reveal a market ready for disruption

Across hundreds of complaints from Trustpilot, OCU, TripAdvisor, Forocoches, Menéame, Mubis, MundoDVD, and expat forums, **seven pain point categories emerge consistently:**

1. **Broken digital infrastructure** — Apps and websites that charge without delivering, switch cinemas/dates, crash during payment, and offer no recourse. Both major chains score below 1.5/5 on Trustpilot.

2. **Zero customer service** — No phone support, email responses taking weeks, rigid no-refund policies even for platform-caused errors. One user summarized: *"No tienen número de teléfono... la única empresa que he visto así."*

3. **VOSE discovery fragmentation** — No unified cross-chain search, inconsistent labeling, limited screenings, outdated blog-based discovery. Growing demand from 6.9M foreign residents and streaming-acclimated locals meets inadequate supply and zero discovery tooling.

4. **Price opacity and hidden fees** — 148% price variation across cinemas, service fees averaging 11%, layered surcharges on subscriptions, no comparison tools. Government intervention is actively underway.

5. **Excessive pre-show advertising** — 15–30 minutes of ads before films. One user clocked 19 minutes of ads plus only 1 minute of trailers. *"Ya pagué 9,40 eurazos por ver la película ¿todavía necesitan recaudar más?"*

6. **Anti-consumer food policies** — Both chains ban outside food despite Spanish courts repeatedly ruling this illegal. Yelmo has been fined **€30,001** in the Basque Country and €25,000 in Almería. Police were called on a couple for bringing pizza into a Yelmo in Valladolid.

7. **Dark-pattern subscriptions** — Cinesa's "Privilegios de Compra" scam charges €18/month after ticket purchases. Cinesa Unlimited requires 3-month minimum commitment with cancellation deliberately hidden: *"En su página web no existe ningún enlace o sección clara dedicada a la cancelación."*

## What this means for CineHop

The research validates **strong product-market fit** for a cinema aggregator in Spain. The gap is not hypothetical — it is explicitly described by users. No platform currently offers cross-chain showtime comparison, unified VOSE discovery, transparent pricing, or multilingual booking. Spain's cinema market serves **72+ million annual visits** generating **€480M+ in revenue**, yet the two dominant digital gatekeepers deliver experiences rated worse than almost any consumer app category.

The most defensible entry points for CineHop would be **VOSE discovery** (an underserved, passionate audience with no adequate tool) and **cross-chain showtime/price comparison** (no competitor exists). The growing foreign population (up significantly year-over-year), streaming-driven original-version preferences among younger Spaniards, and active government pressure on pricing transparency all create favorable tailwinds. The cinema chains' abysmal digital experiences — and apparent inability or unwillingness to fix them — represent a durable competitive moat for any competent third-party solution.
